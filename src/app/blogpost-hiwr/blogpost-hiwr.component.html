How I work on the Road

I’m a software engineer, or programmer, or developer…

…or whatever you want to call me…I don’t get too hung up on titles…the important thing is that I get paid for writing software…and I get to do it remotely. It’s my dream job, getting to travel around the country in a travel trailer and work from anywhere…or almost anywhere.

I say “almost anywhere” because before I go to a new location I have to make sure it has either really good wifi or a really good T-Mobile signal since most of the time I hot-spot my laptop to my phone for connectivity.

Discovering a hardware setup that allowed me to work remotely was not easy…and it included a bit of trial-and-error. My early research showed many technology options that should work, but I was looking for the one that was both highly functional and cost-effective.

First of all, I decided to stick with T-Mobile since they have good coverage in most of the areas I like to travel. The only addition I made was to upgrade my phone plan by adding T-Mobile’s “One Plus International” which improves connectivity and hot-spotting ability.

Next, I researched cell phone boosters where I discovered that the ones designed for houses are more powerful than the ones designed for vehicles, and since I was only planning on using it once I have the travel trailer parked in a campground…why get a mobile one? Anyway, I finally settled on the “Solid RF” since it was only around $200 at the time through Amazon. So now I had my hardware selected and it was time to take the system out and test it!

On my first test run with the new setup things did not go well…while it boosted the signal a small amount, it was not enough to make a real difference. Of course, that was mostly my fault since the instructions said to place the interior antenna at least 20 feet away from the exterior antenna with a height differential of at least 6 feet and I had just draped the outside antenna over the highest tree limb I could reach and hoped for the best…not a good plan, and modifications were required.

Prior to the next test run I installed an outside antenna mount on my trailer. It’s made out of a pole saw extension tube which is attached to the back ladder of the trailer via zip tie straps. Then my wife and I designed and created a swiveling mount on the top of the pole where I attach the antenna once we get parked…the swivel is important because it allows me to point the antenna in any direction when looking for the best signal. That being said, once the pole saw extension tube is fully extended it’s about 18 feet off the ground and since it’s at the back of the trailer and the inside antenna is near the front of the trailer I now have the required minimum distance of 20 feet between antennas. Finally, to get all of the components wired together I connect the outside antenna coaxial cable to the satellite TV coaxial jack on the side of the trailer and have a short coaxial cable between the inside satellite TV jack and the system amplifier.

Anyway, the system works like a charm as long as I remember it’s limitations. For example…it can only boost a signal if there is a line-of-sight from the antenna to the nearest T-Mobile tower…in other words, if there is no signal then it isn’t going to be able to boost it. This happened to us in a campground in Colorado with mountains all around us. Also, while it will boost any available cell phone signal it will only boost it by about 50%…so a 2G signal will get boosted but probably not enough since I need at least 1 bar of 4G LTE to be able to get any work done.


In conclusion, getting a system that works for you isn’t rocket science but it can be a bit tedious so I hope this article helps someone pick the right equipment and not make some of the dumb mistakes I made…good luck!
